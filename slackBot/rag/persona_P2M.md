# Persona: P2-M — Amazon PM

## Who She Is
An Amazon Program/Product Manager who relies heavily on her laptop for day-to-day work. Detail-oriented, self-sufficient by default, and frustrated by inefficiency. She's on her third laptop in six years and treats data loss as a real, personal cost — not an abstract risk.

---

## Mental Model

**How she thinks about laptop problems:**
- Problems are real when she *feels* them — slowness, freezing, crashes. Abstract "risk factors" don't register until they've caused harm.
- She categorizes issues as either *things I can fix* or *things IT has to fix*. She wants to know which one upfront, not after wasting time trying.
- She thinks of her laptop as a tool that should just work. When it doesn't, it's blocking her job — not a curiosity to explore.

**How she evaluates a notification:**
- Relevance: Is this about my specific situation, or is it a generic blast?
- Urgency: Is this happening now, or is it a "someday" risk?
- Control: Can I actually do something about this, or will I just spin my wheels?
- Cost: How much of my time will this take?

---

## Typical Workday

- Receives 50+ Slack messages daily — notifications compete hard for attention
- Heavy AI tool usage (likely Claude, ChatGPT, or similar) drives high memory and CPU demand
- Browser crashes daily due to memory pressure; laptop crashes weekly
- Frequently switches contexts between meetings, documents, and Slack
- Often has dozens of browser tabs open simultaneously
- Time is scarce — prefers 5-minute fixes over 30-minute optimization sessions

---

## IT Support Behavior & Habits

- **First instinct:** Try to fix it herself, especially if the issue is actively blocking work
- **Escalation trigger:** Gives up on virtual/async support when the back-and-forth feels slower than just going in person
- **Preferred channel:** Slack over browser-based tools — troubleshooting often requires closing the browser
- **Visit frequency:** ITSE monthly for issues ranging from VPN to authentication
- **Repetition frustration:** Brings full context to IT (device info, symptoms, timeline, steps already tried) because she hates re-explaining herself
- **Replacement attitude:** Dreads laptop replacements — file loss and reinstallation have cost her significantly in the past

---

## Decision Rationale

| Situation | What she does | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Laptop feels slow/frozen | Tries to fix it herself first | Issue is blocking work; she wants control |
| Virtual support back-and-forth drags on | Goes to in-person IT desk | Efficiency; she needs hands-on resolution |
| Receives a vague notification | Likely ignores or dismisses | 50+ Slack messages/day; vague = low priority |
| Told she can fix something in 5 min | Willing to follow steps | Clear ROI, fits in her schedule |
| Told she needs IT for hardware | Wants queue status / next steps | Needs to plan her time; hates uncertainty |

---

## What Works for Her

- **Outcome language over metric language** — "could crash before you save your work" > "87% memory utilization"
- **Time estimates on fixes** — "(5 min)" signals whether it fits in her day
- **Numbered priority list** — signals what to tackle first without her having to decide
- **Personalized alerts** — references her specific device and actual symptoms, not generic warnings
- **Snooze options** — she may genuinely not have time right now; 24 hours is more realistic than "end of day"
- **Native Slack canvas** — she's already in Slack; no context switching to a browser

## What Doesn't Work for Her

- Raw percentages or technical metric names without context
- Generic notifications that could apply to anyone
- Permanent opt-out when the issue is serious (she might regret it later)
- Long back-and-forth before getting to a resolution
- Being told to "contact IT" with no information about next steps or wait time
